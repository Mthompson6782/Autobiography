# Style Guide — The Improbability of Me

## Voice & POV

### Primary Mode: First-Person Present-Tense Action
Used for all street scenes, confrontations, fights, and high-stakes moments. The reader is *in it*.

> *I feel the pen in my hand. The cap is still on. It doesn't matter. The boy is bigger, and he's already moving.*

### Secondary Mode: First-Person Reflective Past-Tense
Used for analysis, context-setting, and the "engineer's lens" observations. The narrator steps back to assess.

> *Looking back, the failure point was obvious. But you don't see failure points when you're inside the mechanism.*

### The "Accordion" Technique
Every chapter begins with a high-level hook — often a technical or philosophical framing — then *expands* into visceral, ground-level detail. The chapter then *contracts* back to the analytical frame. This mirrors the duality of the narrator: the street kid who became the systems engineer.

---

## Tone Rules

1. **Never sentimental.** The narrator does not ask for pity. He observes. He catalogs. He sometimes laughs.
2. **Technical metaphors are mandatory.** Life events are described through the lens of engineering: failure modes, cascading effects, safety margins, critical reactivity.
3. **Profanity is earned.** Use it for impact, not decoration.
4. **Irony is the through-line.** The man with the rap sheet teaching "Functional Safety." The felon with "Unescorted Access." Let the reader feel the absurdity without pointing at it.
5. **Sensory detail over exposition.** The reader should *smell* Audrey Avenue. *Hear* the hiss of the reactor. *Feel* the weight of the ankle monitor.

---

## Naming Conventions

- **Michael** — used in childhood and family scenes
- **Thompson** — used in professional/corporate contexts
- **The narrator / "I"** — always; never breaks into third person

---

## Chapter Structure

Each chapter follows a loose pattern:
1. **The Hook** — A single image, question, or paradox (1-3 paragraphs)
2. **The Setup** — Context, place, time, stakes
3. **The Action** — The visceral core of the chapter
4. **The Analysis** — The engineer steps back and examines the mechanism
5. **The Bridge** — A line or image that connects to the next chapter's theme

## Scene Types

### Street Scenes (Baltimore / early life)
- Fast, short sentences
- Sensory overload
- Present tense
- No editorializing in the moment

### Military Scenes (Air Force / overseas)
- Mix of camaraderie and tension
- Cultural detail (Okinawa, Thailand)
- Nicknames and unit culture

### Corporate Scenes (Nuclear / Koch / Houston)
- Longer sentences, more analytical
- Jargon deployed with intention
- The absurdity of corporate culture observed clinically

### Medical Scenes (Cancer / VA)
- Clinical language mixed with raw emotion
- The body as a failing mechanism
- Dark humor as survival tool

---

## Do Not

- Use clichés about "overcoming adversity"
- Write inspirational poster language
- Explain the irony — *show it*
- Break the fourth wall
- Write dialogue that sounds like a movie script
- Soften the violence or the consequences
