# Chapter 24: The Double Surgery and the Cold Sweat

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

In engineering, when a system is failing, you run diagnostics. You isolate the fault. You schedule the downtime. You execute the repair. 

You control the process. 

But when the failing system is your own body, control is an illusion. 

The lump I had ignored in Wichita, the one the doctor had told me was "normal," had exploded in size. I went back for a second opinion. The imaging came back, and this time, there was no dismissal. It was clear, undeniable testicular cancer. 

I was devastated. When you hear the word "cancer," your brain automatically jumps to the worst possible outcome. I thought it was the end. I thought about my family, my responsibilities, the house we were trying to build in Bella Vista. The timing felt like a cruel joke—I was finally doing the most important work of my career at MITRE, and my body was shutting down.

The panic was made infinitely worse by a young ultrasound tech. She looked at the screen, her eyes widened, and she looked back at me. 

"It's really bad," she said. 

*What the fuck?* 

When a medical professional says "it's really bad," you assume the cancer is everywhere. You assume it's in your lymph nodes, your lungs, your brain. I was sitting there convinced I was a dead man walking because this tech lacked bedside filter. 

It turned out she was just reacting to the size of the tumor. The cancer had not spread; it was entirely localized. But that single, careless comment shook me to my core.

But the cancer wasn't my only mechanical failure. 

Years of wrestling, lifting heavy, and carrying the physical weight of my life had ground my joints to dust. I had severe osteoarthritis in my left hip. It had deteriorated to bone-on-bone friction. I needed a full hip replacement. 

Two major surgeries. Back-to-back. 

I decided to tackle the hip first. I figured if I was going to be recovering from cancer surgery, I needed to at least be able to walk. 

They sawed off the top of my femur, hammered a titanium spike into my leg, and installed a new socket. It is an incredibly violent surgery. Your body registers it as severe trauma. 

But I am a man who refuses to stop moving. 

The night of the hip replacement, I woke up in the recovery room. I was still floating in that hazy, numb space of heavy anesthesia. My first thought wasn't about my leg or the pain. It was about my team. I didn't want to let MITRE down. I refused to take real time off. I grabbed my laptop, propped myself up in the hospital bed, and started answering emails. I was completely loopy, conducting national security cybersecurity work while my body was still trying to figure out what happened to its skeletal structure. 

Then, the anesthesia wore off. 

The doctors prescribed heavy painkillers—opioids. I refused them. 

Growing up in Baltimore, I had seen exactly what those pills do. I saw people who were tougher than me get swallowed whole by addiction. I had engineered my survival around staying sharp, staying in control. Taking those pills felt like surrendering to the exact kind of chemical chaos I had spent my life escaping. 

So, I decided to raw-dog a full hip replacement. 

I have a high pain tolerance, but this was a different universe of agony. The pain was so intense, so absolute, that my body couldn't even process it as pain anymore. It processed it as freezing cold. 

I lay in bed, shivering violently. My teeth began to chatter. They clacked together so fast and so hard that my wife, Cindy, stood over me, terrified. She thought the physical force of the shivering was going to shatter my teeth in my mouth. 

I fought it for as long as I could. I tried to mentally compartmentalize the agony. But eventually, the physical shock became too dangerous. I conceded. I took the pills. 

I spent six weeks healing. Six weeks of learning how to walk again on a titanium joint, knowing that the second I was strong enough to stand, I had to go right back under the knife. 

Six weeks later, I walked back into the hospital. It was time for the orchiectomy. The cancer had to come out.
