# Chapter 25: Does It Light Up?

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

Here is the truth about having your hip sawed off and replaced with titanium: it really wasn't that bad. 

People hear "full hip replacement" and they grimace, expecting a story of prolonged misery. But they don't understand what it feels like to live with bone-on-bone osteoarthritis. The pain of the arthritis was a constant, grinding agony that gnawed at my sanity every time I took a step. It was a mechanical failure in my own chassis. 

So when I woke up from that surgery—even with the teeth-chattering cold sweats from refusing the pain pills—the relief was immediate. The grinding was gone. The faulty hardware had been replaced. It felt amazing. 

Six weeks later, I went back under the knife for the orchiectomy. 

All things considered, that wasn't too bad either. The fear of cancer is almost always worse than the physical reality of the surgery to remove it. You lie on the table, they put you under, and when you wake up, the tumor is gone. You are lighter. You are missing a piece of yourself, but you are alive. 

Before the surgery, the doctors had asked me a question that perfectly illustrates the disconnect between medical aesthetics and an engineer's mind. 

"Would you like us to put in a prosthetic testicle?" they asked. "For cosmetic reasons."

I actually laughed out loud in the sterile examination room. 

"Does it light up?" I asked. 

They looked at me, confused. 

"No," they said. 

"Then why would I want it?" 

My engineer mind had absolutely zero use for a prosthetic that served no mechanical function. If it doesn't process data, generate power, or at least act as a flashlight, it’s just useless dead weight. I told them to leave the empty space exactly as it was. I didn't need cosmetic symmetry. I needed to live. 

And living was exactly what I planned to do. 

The cancer changed me. For my entire life—from the streets of Baltimore to the cell blocks, to the control rooms of nuclear facilities and the boardrooms of Koch Industries—I had been fighting. I had been surviving. I had been chasing the dollar, trying to outrun the poverty I was born into and the label the government had slapped on me. 

I didn't want to do that anymore. 

I didn't want to work hard just for the sake of working hard. The invincible armor had cracked, and through that crack, a new perspective finally seeped in. I wanted to enjoy my life. I wanted to enjoy my family—my wife Cindy, my kids Alex, Leonidas, and Gabby. My mind shifted from accumulation to appreciation. I was no longer interested in chasing the dollar or the next C-suite title. 

That mindset perfectly aligned with my role at MITRE. 

MITRE wasn't about the hustle; it was about the mission. I loved my time there. I spent almost six years working on some of the most wild, interesting, and highly classified cybersecurity challenges in the world. I was surrounded by brilliant minds, solving problems that actually protected people. It was the purest form of engineering I had ever done. 

The "Kingpin" was dead. The relentless corporate climber was gone. 

What was left was a man with a titanium hip, one testicle, and a profound appreciation for the simple fact that he was still breathing.
