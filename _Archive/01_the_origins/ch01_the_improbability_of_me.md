# Chapter 1: The Improbability of Me

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — from Gemini sessions.*

---

If you look at my résumé today, I make sense.

I am the Director of OT Cybersecurity at the American Bureau of Shipping. I hold a Master’s degree from Johns Hopkins University. I have served as a Chief Technology Officer. I spent five years at MITRE, the government think tank that solves problems for a safer world. I was a Director at Koch Industries, overseeing a $1.7 billion project where failure was not an option. I am a Process Control Engineer who helped shape the standards for nuclear cybersecurity, and I have authored multiple publications for NIST—the federal agency that writes the rules for how America secures its data.

I am a man of systems, logic, and high-level clearance.

But if you pull my other file—the one in the archives of the federal government—you will see a different man.

That file says I was a "Kingpin." It says I was the wholesale distributor of anabolic steroids for the East Coast, the West Coast, and South America. They painted me as some kind of international mastermind, a Tony Montana of testosterone.

I think they overblew that part. I was ambitious, sure. I was arrogant, definitely. But "Kingpin" is a heavy word.

The truth is, I was a kid from Baltimore City, born bordering poverty. I was smart enough to know I wanted out, but arrogant enough to think the rules didn’t apply to me. I joined the Air Force to grow up, get money for school, and see the world. Instead, I saw the inside of a courtroom and the bottom of a hole.

I have been locked in a cage. I have been looked at like I was garbage. I have had cancer try to eat me from the inside out, and I beat that, too.

Most people don't come back from where I’ve been. Statistics say I should be dead, in prison for life, or working a minimum-wage job on the edge of society.

Instead, I rebuilt.

I took the same instincts that kept me alive on Greenmount Avenue and applied them to nuclear reactors. I took the same risk assessment skills I learned in the street and applied them to billion-dollar corporate strategies. I realized that the difference between a "criminal" and a "director" isn't always about intelligence—it's about how you engineer your survival.

This is the story of how I went from a Baltimore street corner to the boardrooms of the biggest industries in the world. It is a story about violence, arrogance, cancer, and redemption.

But before I was an engineer, and before I was a "Kingpin," I was just a lanky fourteen-year-old kid on the #36 bus, trying to get home before the world swallowed me whole.

And the world was hungry.
