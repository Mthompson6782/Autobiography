# Chapter 3.5: The Neighborhood Boys

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

You don't survive a place like South Baltimore alone. You survive in packs. 

My pack was a collection of kids who were all navigating the same broken geometry of the city. We were the products of rowhouses, working-class struggle, and the violent logic of the streets. 

At the center of it all was my best friend, Mike Hammonds. 

Mike was the Paratrooper's son. Like his father, he was a brute. We didn't become friends through shared interests or polite introductions; we became friends through a 7th-grade schoolyard scuffle. Mike was supposed to be the tough guy on the playground. I walked up to him, swept his legs out from under him, and put him on the ground. 

Instead of getting up and trying to kill me, Mike looked up from the dirt and asked, "Do you fish?"

I said yes. From that moment on, we were inseparable. We were the kind of friends who spoke the same language of casual violence and dark humor. I have countless stories about Mike, mostly involving us trying to engineer our way out of trouble we had engineered ourselves into. 

Then there was Chris Smith. He was the one who stood behind me on the stoop in Curtis Bay holding the bayonet. Chris was solid. His mom was a nurse, and his stepdad was a stevedore down at the docks named Frank Door. 

Frank was a legend in our circle. We called him "The Reverend Frank." Whenever he got drunk, which was often, he would stand up and start fiercely preaching to whoever was in the room. Frank was also the man who gave me my moniker. After the incident on North Avenue—where the locking mechanism on my knife failed and I ended up burying the blade into my attacker—Frank caught wind of the story. From that day forward, he refused to call me Michael. 

To The Reverend Frank, I was simply "Blade."

We had Eddie Arron, a good guy whose family was just as poor as ours, but his dad had a relentless hustle and ran a small store. 

We had Artie Buckalew. Artie was probably the poorest kid in our entire group, but you wouldn't know it from hanging out with him. He was a hilarious, good-hearted dude who spoke with a weird, thick speech impediment that captured the perfect, old-school South Baltimore sound. 

And then there was Spunky. 

Spunky was intellectually disabled, but you never brought that up around him. In fact, Spunky possessed a strange, aggressive hatred for other mentally disabled people. He also possessed a terrifying amount of physical strength and zero impulse control. Once, Spunky got into a dispute with a local family called the McFarlands. It didn't end with a shouting match. In a single day, Spunky systematically tracked down and beat the absolute shit out of the little brother, the older brother, and the father. He dismantled an entire family tree before the sun went down. 

But not everyone in the pack was defined by brute force. Some were defined by what they failed to become. 

Big Ed, who we affectionately called "Big Head," went to high school with me at Baltimore City College HS. Ed was a dreamer. He talked a big game and desperately wanted to be perceived as a tough guy, but in reality, he was a coward. He would fold when the pressure was on. 

But his cowardice wasn't his tragedy. His tragedy was his brain. 

Big Ed had a brilliant mathematical mind. To this day, I believe he was actually smarter than I am. His raw processing power was staggering. But he lacked the drive, the discipline, and the execution to apply it to anything meaningful. He let the environment dictate his trajectory instead of engineering his own way out. 

Today, while I secure global corporate infrastructure, Big Ed works as a deli clerk, slicing lunch meat at a grocery store. Tremendous, staggering wasted potential. 

Looking back, that crew was a perfect cross-section of Baltimore. We were a mix of killers, dreamers, hustlers, and casualties. We learned how to fight from Paratroopers, got our nicknames from drunken stevedores, and watched geniuses slice bologna because they couldn't figure out how to escape. 

They were my first real team. And they taught me exactly who I wanted to be, and exactly who I refused to become.
