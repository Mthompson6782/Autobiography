# Chapter 4: Audrey Avenue

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

Before the Paratrooper, before Johnson Street, and before the knives came out on Greenmount Avenue, there was Audrey Avenue. 

Audrey Avenue was in Brooklyn, a neighborhood in South Baltimore. In the geography of the city, Brooklyn is where you go when you slide. It’s a purgatory of low-cost apartments and a shittier class of people. When you end up in Brooklyn, you are there for one of two reasons: you are there to completely give up, or you are there to get your shit together. 

My dad was trying to get his shit together. 

Just like my mother lacked maternal instincts, my dad was young and figuring out the father thing as we went. He did his best. We lived in a row of old brick rowhouses that had been chopped up and converted into cheap, run-down apartments. Our place was small, shitty, and stripped of anything resembling luxury. 

But a neighborhood is defined by its ecosystem, and Audrey Avenue had an ecosystem that bordered on the surreal. The people who lived there were a strange mix of the broken and the bizarre. 

For the most part, the people were nice. There was a lady who worked sanitation—we just called her the garbage lady—who used to comb through the trash on her routes. Whenever she found toys that people in better zip codes had thrown away, she would bring them home and give them to us. It was a small act of kindness in a place that didn't have much of it to spare. 

Then there were the Heberles. They were poor, but they were good people. The older brother knew karate and carried himself like a total badass. The younger brother, Tim, was my age. Tim was way behind academically, struggling with learning disabilities that the neighborhood wasn't equipped to handle, but we hung out anyway. 

And then there were Nancy and Sandy. 

If Audrey Avenue was purgatory, Nancy and Sandy were the wardens. They were two enormously fat sisters who lived on the block, and they operated one of the most terrifying and inexplicable setups I have ever seen. 

They literally kept slaves. 

There were two other girls, Misty and Pam. Nancy claimed ownership over Misty, and Sandy claimed ownership over Pam. It wasn't a metaphor. These girls were actual slaves to these two massive women, forced to serve them and endure whatever psychological or physical punishments they decided to dish out. 

I never understood the mechanics of how that arrangement started, or why the girls didn't just walk away. The psychology of poverty and abuse can trap people in invisible cages. The cruelty was casual and normalized. I remember one day, Nancy’s brother, J.R., decided Misty needed to be punished. He forced her to repeatedly bang her own head against a concrete floor. 

I think Misty and Pam eventually escaped, but the memory of them stuck with me. 

Audrey Avenue was a masterclass in the extremes of human nature. You had a woman digging toys out of the garbage to make poor kids smile, living right down the street from people enforcing a concrete-floor slave state. 

It was a chaotic, bizarre environment, but my dad kept us moving forward. He used Brooklyn for what it was meant to be used for: a place to get his shit together. Eventually, we climbed out of the slide, left Audrey Avenue behind, and moved up to Johnson Street. 

But Brooklyn left its mark. It taught me early on that the world isn't just divided into good and evil. Sometimes, it’s just a neighborhood where the garbage lady and the monsters share the exact same sidewalk.
