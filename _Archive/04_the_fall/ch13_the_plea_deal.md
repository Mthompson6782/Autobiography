# Chapter 13: The Plea Deal

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

When the federal government kicks down your door, you assume you are going to federal prison. The feds have a 98% conviction rate for a reason—they don't go to trial unless they have a slam dunk. 

But as the dust settled on my arrest, a different reality started to emerge. The feds looked at the case the local police had built. They looked at the evidence handling. They looked at the procedures. What they saw was classic Baltimore: sloppy and corrupt. 

The feds realized the case wouldn't hold up under the microscopic scrutiny of a federal courtroom. So, they quietly dropped their charges and handed the mess over to the State of Maryland. 

The State of Maryland has significantly lower standards. 

I spent about four months locked inside as the case slowly ground its way through the judicial system. If you want to test a man’s psychological resilience, put him in a concrete box for four months while the state tries to figure out how many decades of his life they want to take away. 

Eventually, the state prosecutors came to the table. They knew their case was built on a shaky, corrupt foundation, but they also knew they had the leverage of the system behind them. They gave me an ultimatum. 

"If you fight this in court," they told me, "and you lose, we will stack the charges. We will make sure you never see the outside again."

It was a math problem. 

On one side of the equation was my pride, my innocence regarding the scale of the operation, and the knowledge that the police had butchered the case. On the other side was the very real possibility of spending the best years of my life rotting in a cell because a Maryland judge decided to make an example out of a so-called "Kingpin."

I did what any engineer does when faced with an unacceptable level of risk: I mitigated it. 

I took the plea deal. 

The sentence was heavy, but it wasn't a coffin. I received a ten-year suspended sentence, three years of probation, and eighteen months of house arrest. 

I walked out of lockup after four months with a federal record and an ankle monitor strapped to my leg. I was legally confined to my house. I couldn't leave without permission. The government was tracking my every movement. I had lost my freedom, my money, and my reputation. 

But I had a house. I had a wife. I had my brain. 

I was on house arrest, but I wasn't dead. The state thought they had permanently sidelined me, turning me into a statistic—another kid from Baltimore who got caught up and ruined his life. They thought the ankle monitor was the end of the story. 

They didn't realize it was just the beginning of the rebuild.
