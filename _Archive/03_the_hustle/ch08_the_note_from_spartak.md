# Chapter 8: The Note from Spartak

---

I didn't start building an international drug distribution network while I was wearing a uniform. I wasn't that stupid. 

When I was stationed at Kadena Air Base in Okinawa, I was focused on the military and my own physical development. I spent my off-duty hours at a dojo off Highway 58, training in Kyokushin Karate—one of the hardest, most punishing styles of martial arts on the planet. I was channeling the violence of Baltimore into discipline. 

In 1999, my time in the Air Force came to an end. I took off the uniform, packed up my life, and headed back stateside as a civilian. 

For a while, I did exactly what you're supposed to do. I got a job at the airport. I started taking college classes. I bought my first house. I was just a guy in his early twenties, doing early-twenties guy stuff. I was arrogant, sure, and I was definitely acting like an asshole sometimes, but I was genuinely trying to put a legitimate life together. I had the aerospace electrical training from the military, and I had the discipline to work. I was engineering a normal life. 

But sometimes, the past leaves a breadcrumb that alters the trajectory of your entire future. 

One day, I was going through some of my old military gear and paperwork. Tucked away among the souvenirs and deployment documents, I found a piece of paper. 

It was the note from Spartak. 

Spartak Todorov was the Eastern European contact I had met in Thailand during Operation Cobra Gold. He was the guy who had introduced me to the actual, unadulterated power of pharmaceutical-grade steroids. He had scribbled his contact information on that piece of paper and handed it to me years ago. 

I sat there looking at the note. I thought about the physical transformation I had undergone in Thailand. I thought about the culture of bodybuilding, powerlifting, and athletics back in the States, and how desperately people wanted what Spartak had access to. 

I didn't immediately decide to become a "Kingpin." It was just a calculation. I saw an inefficiency in the market: supply was localized in Eastern Europe and Asia, and demand was massive in America. I had the contact. I had the logistical understanding of how things moved across borders from my time at the airport and in the military. 

I picked up the phone and reached out to Spartak. 

That single phone call was the ignition switch. It didn't start as an international empire. It started as a small, highly calculated hustle. But I don't know how to do things halfway. The same obsessive drive that made me read encyclopedias cover-to-cover, the same strategic mindset that allowed me to survive Greenmount Avenue, was suddenly applied to the black market. 

I started moving product. And because I understood systems, I built a system that was faster, more reliable, and more secure than anyone else's. 

I wasn't just working at the airport anymore. The hustle had officially begun.
