# Chapter 10: The Customs Guy and the Western Union

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

If you read the federal indictment, you would think I was running a cartel. You would picture me sitting in a mansion, counting stacks of untraceable cash while armed guards patrolled the perimeter. 

The government has a vested interest in blowing things out of proportion. They need "Kingpins" to justify their budgets. But the reality of my operation was aggressively mundane. 

There was no big money. It was modest. I wasn't buying sports cars or flying on private jets. The entire financial backbone of the operation was just me standing in line at various Western Union counters, filling out transfer slips. I used basic operational security—I changed up which Western Union locations I used to wire the money back to Spartak, and I rotated the post offices where I dropped off the domestic packages. It was routine. It was quiet. 

And honestly, I didn't think it was that serious. 

You have to look at the era. It was the late 1990s and early 2000s. Mark McGwire and Sammy Sosa were on television every night, built like comic book characters, shattering home run records while the entire country cheered. Barry Bonds’ head was physically expanding in real-time. The guys in the WWF were massive. NFL linebackers looked like silverbacks. Everywhere you looked, the absolute pinnacle of American culture and athletics was openly fueled by the exact same chemicals I was shipping in Happy Birthday cans. 

Half the guys in my local gym were on cycle. It was normalized. 

In my mind, I wasn't trafficking narcotics; I was just providing logistical support for a culturally accepted performance enhancer. I had absolutely no idea the federal government was going to treat me like Pablo Escobar. 

Because I didn't think I was a high-value target, I wasn't looking over my shoulder. I felt completely insulated. My operational security on the domestic side was solid. The packages went out, the money went through Western Union, and the cycle repeated. 

But in engineering, it doesn't matter how solid your node is if an upstream dependency fails. 

The vulnerability wasn't in Baltimore. The vulnerability was in Customs. 

It turns out that Spartak's Eastern European operation was massive, and to get that sheer volume of product into the United States, they had compromised the border. They had a U.S. Customs agent on the take. For a long time, that agent had been looking the other way, ensuring the shipments sailed through the checkpoints untouched. 

But the feds eventually caught the dirty Customs guy. 

When you catch a dirty federal agent, they know exactly how the system works. They know the mandatory minimums. They know that the only way to save themselves is to give up everyone else. 

The Customs agent flipped. He ratted out the entire operation. He handed the government the shipping manifests, the routing information, and the destination addresses. 

The federal government didn't have to launch a sophisticated wiretap operation to find me. They just followed the paper trail the Customs guy handed them. 

I was completely blindsided. One day I was a guy working at the airport, wiring modest sums of money through Western Union and going to the gym. The next day, the entire weight of the United States federal government came crashing down on my front door.
