# Epilogue: The Calculation

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

Even when you stop chasing the dollar, you never stop being an engineer. You never stop looking at the math. 

I loved MITRE. I believed in the mission. But the landscape was shifting. Funding was getting tight due to new government initiatives and budget constraints, and the organization had to start shedding weight. 

They announced a Reduction in Force (RIF). 

I survived the first round of layoffs. I survived the second round, too. But while everyone else was breathing sighs of relief, I was looking at the severance package they were offering. It was six months of pay with full benefits. To most people, a layoff is a disaster. To someone who grew up surviving the streets of Baltimore and dodging federal indictments, six months of guaranteed runway is a massive tactical advantage. 

I actually volunteered to be laid off during the second round. They rejected my request. I was too valuable to the projects I was on. 

I made it through the third round. Then, the President of MITRE put out a call asking for voluntary separations to prevent further involuntary cuts. 

I raised my hand again. This time, they let me go. 

It was a perfectly calculated exit. I took that massive severance package and immediately secured an interim role as the Director of OT Cybersecurity at the American Bureau of Shipping (ABS) for six months. Because I had income flowing from ABS, I didn't need the MITRE money to live on. 

I took every single dollar of that MITRE severance payout and dumped it straight into VOO—an S&P 500 index fund. 

The market surged. I made a small fortune simply by understanding leverage and timing. It was the same instinct that told me when to swing first on Greenmount Avenue, just applied to a Vanguard portfolio. 

With that financial cushion secured, I had the ultimate luxury: the ability to wait for the perfect opportunity. I didn't have to move. I didn't have to chase a title in Houston or Wichita. We had built our house in Northwest Arkansas. We were rooted. 

Then, the opportunity appeared right in my backyard. 

Walmart—the largest retailer on the planet, headquartered right here in NWA—was looking for a Principal OT Cyber Engineer. They needed someone who understood how to secure massive, complex physical systems at a global scale. They needed someone who understood risk, failure points, and resilience. 

I grabbed it. 

Which brings me to today. 

I sit at my desk in Northwest Arkansas, securing the infrastructure for a company that moves the global economy. I go home to a house I built, to a wife who stood by me through the darkest hours, and to kids who will never have to know what it feels like to wait for a bus on a corner where grown men want to kill you. 

When I look back at the file the government built on me—the one that labeled me a Kingpin, the one that said I was destined for a cage—it almost feels like it belongs to someone else. Almost. 

But I know it belongs to me. I survived the rap sheet to build the résumé. 

I am a man constructed from mechanical failures. Every time a system broke down—a folding knife lock, a pharmaceutical process, a titanium hip, or a localized tumor—I analyzed the fault. I stored the information. I adjusted the design. 

The world is a violent, unpredictable machine. It will try to break you. It will test your joints, your freedom, and your cells. 

But if you understand the mechanics of survival, you can rebuild from anything. You can engineer your own improbability.
