# Chapter 18: Spartan Technology

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

I knew Johnny Coxon from FMC. He followed me to TAI Engineering, where he was the Instrumentation & Control Engineering Manager before me. Johnny was brilliant, volatile, and absolutely incapable of tolerating corporate stupidity. 

One day, he had enough. He rage-quit.

It wasn't a quiet, dignified resignation. It was a Johnny Coxon resignation—loud, profane, and memorable. TAI wasn't upset about it. In fact, they had been hoping for it. The moment Johnny walked out the door, they promoted me into his position. I became the I&C Division Manager, designing and commissioning control systems for biotech and pharmaceutical manufacturing facilities.

But Johnny and I stayed in touch. We were cut from similar cloth—two engineers who understood that the real money wasn't in working for someone else. It was in owning the machine.

We hatched a plan. We were going to start our own company.

By this time, I had moved from TAI to BGE/Exelon. I was stationed at Spring Gardens, re-engineering the entire gas distribution system for Baltimore and working on the plant's LNG control systems. It was serious, critical infrastructure work—the kind of systems where a miscalculation doesn't just cost money, it levels a city block.

But I wasn't content with one job. The old hustle instinct—the same drive that had gotten me in trouble with the Happy Birthday cans—was still alive. Only now, it was pointed in a legitimate direction.

By day, I was an engineer at Exelon, redesigning the gas infrastructure for one of the largest cities on the East Coast.

By night, I was building Spartan Technology.

Johnny and I bootstrapped the entire thing. No venture capital. No angel investors. No trust fund. Just two guys with engineering backgrounds, a phone, and the audacity to cold-call Fortune 100 industrial clients and tell them we could secure their systems better than anyone else.

We connected with a few key clients early on, and the work spoke for itself. We weren't selling snake oil or PowerPoint presentations. We were delivering actual, hands-on calibration and instrumentation & control engineering across oil and gas, power, and chemical industries. We calibrated sensors, designed control loops, commissioned systems, and performed the precise, technical work that keeps industrial plants running within their operating parameters.

The growth was violent. We went from zero to one million dollars in revenue in about two years. Pure bootstrapping. Every dollar we earned went right back into the machine. We were lean, fast, and hungry.

Johnny was the chaos engine. He was the guy who would walk into a client meeting, say something completely insane, and somehow close the deal because the client respected the honesty. He was a fucking nut, but he was *my* fucking nut. We complemented each other perfectly: I was the systems thinker, the architect, the guy who built the frameworks. Johnny was the accelerant, the guy who lit the match and made things happen at a speed that terrified our competitors.

Running Spartan while working full-time at Exelon was exhausting. I was burning the candle at both ends, sleeping four or five hours a night, and running on caffeine and the sheer adrenaline of building something from nothing. But it was the most alive I had felt since the streets of Baltimore.

The difference was, this time, the hustle was legal. The intelligence and guile were being applied to a legitimate enterprise. I was a convicted felon who had started his own consulting firm and was pulling in seven figures. The State of Maryland had labeled me a Kingpin and tried to bury me. Instead, I had become a CEO.

Spartan Technology was proof of concept. It proved that the same instincts that had built an international steroid distribution network could build a legitimate business—faster, cleaner, and without the risk of a federal raid.

But the universe has a way of presenting opportunities that are too massive to ignore. Koch Industries came calling with a $1.7 billion project, and the scale of that opportunity dwarfed what Spartan could offer.

It was time to make the jump. But Spartan Technology would always be the chapter where I proved—to myself and to the world—that the Kingpin label was just a label. The real title was Founder.
