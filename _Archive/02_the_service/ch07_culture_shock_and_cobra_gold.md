# Chapter 4: Culture Shock and Cobra Gold

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — from Gemini sessions.*

---

If Baltimore was a lesson in how to survive chaos, Japan was a lesson in how to live in order.

Okinawa felt like a different planet. The humidity was heavy, the neon lights of Gate 2 Street were blinding, and the air smelled of ocean salt and JP-8 jet fuel. But the biggest shock wasn't the weather or the food.

It was the trust.

I was riding my bike out near Gate 2 Street one afternoon. I stopped at a red light, and in my peripheral vision, I saw movement. A local man was running full sprint toward me, screaming.

“Chotto matte kudasai! Chotto matte kudasai!”

My Baltimore brain didn't translate the Japanese. It only translated the tone: Pursuit.

Instinct took the wheel. I slammed on the pedals. I didn't know what he wanted, but I knew that in my old neighborhood, if someone is chasing you, you don't stick around to find out why. I was mashing the gears, trying to get separation.

He kept coming. He was fast. I finally hit a bottleneck, and he caught up, breathless, chest heaving.

I braced myself. I was ready to throw the bike and square up.

He reached out his hand. He wasn't holding a weapon. He was holding my wallet.

It had fallen out of my pocket a block back. This man had sprinted a quarter-mile in the humidity just to make sure the foreigner didn't lose his ID and cash.

I took the wallet, stunned. In Baltimore, that wallet would have been gone before it hit the pavement. Here, people returned it. It was alien to me. It took a long time for my nervous system to uncoil, to realize that not everyone was trying to take something from me.

But the military is still the military, and you can’t escape human nature completely.

My boss at the time was a newly minted Staff Sergeant. He had just squeaked by to get his stripe before the Buck Sergeant rank was phased out. He was feeling himself. He thought the extra stripe made him a tough guy.

One night in the barracks, he got drunk. He decided he was going to be "Billy Badass" at my expense. He started talking shit, getting loud, trying to son me in front of the other guys.

I talked shit back.

He didn't like that. He threw a punch. It was the weakest, most uncoordinated loop I had ever seen. It was slow motion.

I ducked under it, took his back, and gently sunk in the hooks. I wrapped my arm around his neck for a rear naked choke. But I didn't squeeze. I just held him there, controlling him, giving him a chance to cool off.

"I’m going to break your legs!" he screamed.

He was one of those "I see red and bodies hit the floor" weirdos. I knew he wasn't going to break anything.

"You aren't doing anything from here," I whispered.

He tried to raise his elbows high and slam them down into my thighs to break my grip. It was pathetic. It tickled more than it hurt. I actually laughed.

"Okay," I said. "Nap time."

I squeezed. The blood flow to his brain stopped. He went limp in seconds. I let him slide to the floor and walked away.

My friend saw the whole thing and lost his mind. "Whoa! You just choked out the Staff Sergeant!"

He told the story to everyone. That was the mistake. You can fight, but you can't embarrass the chain of command.

I got called in to "Stand Before The Man." The higher-ups weren't mad about the fight—they knew the Sergeant was drunk and out of line—but they couldn't have the story circulating. It undermined authority.

We came to an understanding. I shut up about it, and nothing went on my record. Me and the Staff Sergeant actually became good friends after that. As is often the case, violence clears the air.

Then came Operation Cobra Gold.

We deployed to Thailand for the massive joint exercise. If I thought I knew poverty from West Baltimore, Thailand showed me I knew nothing. This was real poverty. Dirt floors, tin roofs, people with absolutely zero upward mobility. In the US, you can hustle your way out. There, the ceiling was made of concrete. It humbled me.

One night, I was sitting at an open-air Muay Thai bar. The local fighters—small, wiry, made of iron—were in the ring, chopping each other down.

Sitting next to me was a monster.

He looked like Ivan Drago from Rocky IV. Blonde, massive, sculpted. He was debating getting in the ring to fight one of the Thais. A lot of tourists do this. It rarely ends well.

"They can kick me here," he said, pointing to his legs. "No problem."

He pointed to his abs. "They can kick me here. No problem."

Then he pointed to his head. "But they can’t kick me here."

I nodded. I watched the Thais dismantle people for a living. I figured Drago was about to learn a lesson in physics, but I was curious about how he got that big.

"What’s your lifting regimen look like?" I asked.

He smiled. It was a conspiratorial smile.

"You need some help," he said. "Steroids."

I blinked. I was active duty. I was 2A656. I had a clearance.

"I’m interested," I said, "but I can't. I’m in the military. I can't get dinged on a urinalysis."

"If you change your mind," he said, scribbling on a piece of paper, "write me. I will hook you up."

He handed me the slip. Spartak Todorov.

I put the paper in my pocket. I probably should have thrown it away. I should have left it in that bar in Thailand. But I didn't. I stuck it in a drawer with my other souvenirs when I got back to Okinawa.

I didn't look at it for years.

Fast forward. I was out of the Air Force. I was back in the States, trying to figure out my next move. I was into weightlifting again, packing on size, trying to see how far I could push my genetics.

One day, I was rummaging through an old box of stuff from my time in Japan.

There it was. The slip of paper.

Spartak Todorov.

I wrote him a letter.

A few weeks later, I got a reply. It contained a Hushmail address—one of those encrypted, secure email services—and directions on how to proceed.

I sat down at my computer. I typed the address.

I didn't know it yet, but I had just initialized the sequence that would turn me into a "Kingpin."
