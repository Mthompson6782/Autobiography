# Chapter 23: Takeda, MITRE, and the Airbnbs

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — pending review.*

---

The Great American Loop was the reset I needed. Four months on the road with the family, staring at Crater Lake and breathing the air in Montana, washed the corporate grime of the CETO job off me. 

But a man like me doesn’t stay idle for long. I had the monkey swinging from branch to branch—I had a job lined up before we even left the driveway for the road trip. I just told them I needed a few weeks to tie up loose ends. 

I went back to consulting. I didn't want to be a Director or a Chief Officer managing a sales pipeline anymore. I wanted to be an individual contributor. I wanted to get my hands back on the technology. 

I took a role doing OT Cybersecurity for Capgemini. They put me on a massive account: Takeda, the Japanese multinational pharmaceutical and biopharmaceutical company. My job was to put together their entire OT cyber program, securing the systems that manufactured life-saving biotech. 

And here is where the universe loves to close a loop. 

When I was a kid in Baltimore trying to escape the chaos of Greenmount Avenue, I studied Japanese. I started in the 6th grade, carrying it with me into the Air Force when I deployed to Okinawa. It was my intellectual escape hatch. Now, decades later, I was sitting across the table from Japanese executives at a massive pharma corporation.

When I started speaking to them in their native language, the dynamic shifted immediately. In consulting, competence gets you in the door, but cultural fluency gets you the keys to the castle. The Japanese executives loved me. It was a perfect fit. I spent about two years locking down their infrastructure and building their cyber program. 

But then, MITRE called. 

MITRE is the pinnacle for someone in my field. It’s a federally funded research and development center (FFRDC)—a government think tank that solves the hardest problems for the defense and intelligence communities. It’s where the smartest people in the room go to do work that actually matters for the safety of the world. 

I couldn't say no. I quit Capgemini and accepted the role at MITRE. 

But taking the job meant a massive geographic shift. We were leaving Houston behind. We sold our house and set our sights on Northwest Arkansas—specifically, Bella Vista. 

It was a bold move. We sold the Houston house before our new house in Arkansas was even built. So, as I stepped into this elite government think tank role, my family and I were technically homeless. We spent our time bouncing from one Airbnb to another in Northwest Arkansas, living out of suitcases while we waited for our house to be finished. 

It was an adventure, chaotic but exciting. We were building a new life in the Ozarks, and I was doing work at the highest levels of national security. 

But I was still ignoring the passenger that had been riding with me since Wichita. 

The lump hadn't gone away. It was still growing. And the bill for ignoring it was finally about to come due.
