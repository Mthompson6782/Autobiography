# Chapter 22: The Golden Handcuffs and the Great Escape

> *STATUS: DRAFTED — from Gemini sessions.*

---

I went to Houston to be a Chief Engineer. I found out quickly that titles are cheap.

The **CETO** role (Chief Engineer and Technology Officer) sounded impressive on a business card. In reality, it was a sales grinder. They didn't want my "Intelligence and Guile"; they wanted me to shake hands, kiss babies, and close deals.

I hated it. It pulled me away from the technology, away from the problem-solving, and away from the things that actually stimulated my brain. I was burnt out within months.

But I am a professional. I didn't just quit. I built them a legacy before I left.

I looked at their portfolio and realized they were leaving money on the table. I created a **Calibration Business** from scratch—leveraging my old Northrop and FMC roots. It became one of their biggest revenue streams. I made them a fortune, while I was miserable.

I did it for exactly one year. On the 365th day, I walked.

**Worst. Job. Ever.**

But there was something darker happening in the background.

Back in Wichita, before I even left for Houston, I had noticed the lump. It was bigger than the "pencil tip" from years ago. I did the responsible thing. I went to the doctor.

He examined me. *"I can't feel anything,"* he said. *"It's normal. You're fine."*

He sent me home.

He was wrong.

While I was grinding through the sales meetings in Houston, the lump was growing. I ignored it because the doctor had told me it was nothing, and because I was too burnt out to argue with my own body.

When I quit the CETO job, I decided I needed a reset. I needed to scrub the corporate slime off my soul.

I took four months off. I packed the family into the car, and we just drove.

We did the **Great American Loop**.

- **Crater Lake:** Staring into that deep, impossible blue.
- **Yellowstone:** Watching the earth vent its own pressure.
- **Montana:** The Big Sky country where you feel small.
- **North Dakota:** The quiet, rugged expanse.

It was magical. It was freedom. I was unemployed, unburdened, and surrounded by my family.

But the whole time, as we drove through these breathtaking landscapes, a passenger was riding with us. The lump was getting bigger. The "normal" diagnosis from Wichita was ticking away like a time bomb in the passenger seat.
